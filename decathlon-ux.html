Context

This wasn't an assigned task. My mentor gave me open-ended space to find problems independently, and I started noticing friction points in Decathlon's WeChat Mini Program while using it as a normal customer. The more I looked, the more I found.

I proposed running a structured benchmarking exercise — mapping Decathlon's full purchase and returns experience against JD 1P, JD 3P, Tmall, and several sports brand Mini Programs. The goal: identify concrete, prioritizable UX gaps that could be turned into a formal project proposal.

One constraint worth naming

No internal user behavior data was available — no heatmaps, no conversion funnel. I flagged this to my mentor. His judgment: the pain points were obvious enough from competitive observation to proceed without it. I'd push harder for the data next time.

Research Method

Full-funnel competitive benchmarking across four reference platforms, covering every touchpoint from product discovery through after-sales:

  • JD 1P — JD's own-branded store, highest operational standard
  • JD 3P — Third-party merchants on JD, more analogous to Decathlon's setup
  • Tmall flagship stores — Benchmark for Mini Program experience quality
  • Sports / outdoor brand Mini Programs — Direct category competitors

For each platform, I went through the complete user journey: product detail page (PDP), logistics tracking, and after-sales / returns flow. Every gap was documented with screenshots and written analysis.

Total pain points identified: 16, spanning both the forward purchase flow and reverse returns flow.

Prioritization Framework

Not all 16 pain points deserved equal attention. I built a customized Value-Effort Matrix with dual-dimension weighted scoring to sort them:

  • Consumer need intensity (0–5): How much does this pain point actually affect the user's experience and purchasing decision?
  • Business value (0–5): What is the commercial impact — conversion lift, return rate reduction, brand perception?

The weighted total score determined quadrant placement. I held two alignment meetings with the team's dedicated Project Manager to validate that the quadrant placements reflected operational reality, not just my own assessment.

Key Decision: Courier Pickup Deprioritized

This was the most contentious prioritization call. My mentor initially wanted to push self-service return with courier pickup into the sprint — it's a visible, customer-facing feature that signals Decathlon takes returns seriously.

I argued against it with two reasons:

  1. Implementation cost: Courier pickup requires reverse logistics API integration and new courier partnership agreements. The short-term development cost is high relative to the immediate ROI.
  2. Business impact: Reducing friction in the returns flow would likely spike return rates in the short term, increasing reverse logistics costs before the infrastructure is ready to absorb that volume efficiently.

My recommendation: necessary long-term, wrong timing. Keep in the roadmap at low priority. My mentor agreed.

How I framed the argument

I didn't tell my mentor he was wrong. I showed the cost analysis and the likely return-rate impact, then asked: "Does the short-term ROI justify this ahead of the quick wins?" The math answered the question.

Standout Pain Points

A few examples that illustrate the kind of gaps being flagged:

  • 01 SKU naming inconsistency. Product names in the Mini Program didn't match how products appeared in off-platform seeding content (Xiaohongshu, Douyin). Users who arrived from off-platform couldn't find the product they'd seen. Dual impact: conversion and brand consistency.
  • 02 No logistics ETA display. After placing an order, users had no visibility into when to expect delivery. JD shows estimated arrival at the product page and order confirmation level. Low development cost, high UX impact — this went into the sprint.
  • 03 After-sales entry point buried. Users trying to initiate a return or file a complaint couldn't find the entry point without significant searching. Competitor platforms surface this at the order level. Creates customer service volume for issues that should be self-serviceable.
  • 04 No self-service courier pickup for returns. JD 1P offers this natively. Deprioritized for now, but a meaningful gap relative to category expectations — especially for sports equipment returns where items are heavy or awkward to carry.

Output

The project produced two deliverables:

  • Prioritized requirements document — 16 pain points mapped into the Value-Effort quadrants, with each high-priority item detailed enough to be scoped for a sprint. High-priority items pushed directly into development; lower-priority items documented in the backlog.
  • BRD: delivery radius expansion — A separate but related document proposing to expand the Mini Program's delivery radius from 3km to 10km, with projected annual GMV uplift of ¥6M+.

Both documents were aligned with my mentor and the team's PM before being submitted for formal project approval.

What I'd do differently

I used competitive benchmarking as a proxy for user research because internal behavioral data wasn't available. It worked well enough to get the project approved — but the ideal validation would include heatmap data and conversion funnel analysis to test whether my prioritization matches where users are actually dropping off. Next time, I'd push harder for that data before finalizing the matrix.
