CNY Campaign

Company Mayora Food

Platform Meituan Flash Sale

Context

This was Mayora's first-ever instant retail campaign. No SOP, no historical benchmark data, no prior Meituan campaign to reference. I was handed a ¥230K budget and asked to make it work for Chinese New Year — the highest-stakes gifting window of the year.

Two product lines ran in parallel: gift boxes (the volume driver, tied to CNY gifting behavior) and new products (Kopiko variants, using the holiday as a launch window). I was responsible for the day-to-day execution — audience targeting strategy, budget allocation, optimization decisions, and reporting. My line manager set the macro direction; everything below that was mine to figure out.

Starting condition

Zero historical data. Zero SOP. First time Mayora ran an instant retail campaign of any kind.

Budget Structure

Total approved budget: ¥230K across two mechanics — RTB ad placements and platform vouchers.

RTB and vouchers serve different functions. RTB is about finding the right people — placing your ad in front of high-intent audiences through real-time bidding. Vouchers give those people a reason to convert. Running both in parallel means you're solving for both reach quality and conversion rate simultaneously.

The voucher line significantly underspent against budget. This was a deliberate call by the team — participating too aggressively in voucher mechanics during a first campaign would have made it harder to isolate what was actually driving sales.

Audience Targeting Strategy

With no prior data, every audience decision started as a hypothesis. I mapped each segment to a specific behavioral logic, then planned to validate mid-campaign and cut anything that wasn't working.

Audience Segment Hypothesis Outcome
Cookie / biscuit buyers Highest category affinity — lowest conversion barrier, core base Kept
Gift-giving & care CNY gifting occasion + gift box = direct intent match for "send as gift" use case Kept + scaled
Existing customers Brand familiarity drives repurchase during CNY; re-engagement cost is low Kept
Dairy transaction buyers Hypothesis: biscuits + milk is a common consumption pairing — adjacent category crossover Cut (Week 1)

CPC objective		 Split out post-campaign as separate low-budget test on CPC bidding Test

The dairy hypothesis turned out to be wrong. Adjacent category logic sounded reasonable but didn't hold up in practice — the incremental ROI was insufficient to justify continued spend. We cut it in Week 1 and reallocated the budget to the segments that were already outperforming.

Standout ROI — gift box line

Existing customers: ROI 38.7  ·  Cookie buyers: ROI 56.4  ·  Gift-giving & care: ROI 7.3

Two Rounds of Optimization

The campaign ran two structured optimization rounds, each triggered by accumulated performance data rather than a fixed calendar date.

Round 1 Precision Pivot Week 1
  • Gift box cities expanded from 3 to 5 (added Nanjing, Guangzhou)
  • Switched from broad targeting to precision insight plans across both lines
  • Moved gift box bidding to CPM mode for better impression control
  • Eliminated dairy transaction audience — ROI insufficient, budget reallocated to gift-giving segment
Round 2 Sprint Ramp Jan 13–17 (peak CNY window)
  • Gift box GMV daily caps scaled: ¥500 → ¥800 → ¥1,000 → ¥3,000
  • New product daily caps scaled: ¥1,000 → ¥1,500 → ¥2,000
  • Ad delivery hours extended from afternoon-only to full day (07:00–24:00)
  • Continued eliminating low-performing segments; concentrated spend on biscuit buyers and gift-giving audiences

Post-Campaign Wind-Down

After peak, gift box plans were fully paused (budget utilized). New products shifted to reduced frequency — hours narrowed back to 14:00–22:00, daily budget reduced to ¥250. Only top-performing audiences (biscuit buyers, home-living segment) were retained.

The 600% growth figure compares sales during the CNY window against the daily average baseline in January before the campaign launched — not a year-over-year comparison. The validation method cross-checked against the 2024 same-period data and isolated campaign-attributed sales from organic sales to control for seasonal uplift.

Stage 1 ROI (no daily cap, D-16 to D+5): 1.11. Stage 2 ROI (¥1,500 cap, D+6 to D+20): 1.43. Both below the gift box line, which was expected — this was a first-time launch with no brand recognition on the platform.

Combined Assessment

Against actual spend of ¥162K (vouchers underspent), combined ROI was approximately 3.81 — above the 3.7 target. Against the full approved budget of ¥230K, combined ROI would have been approximately 2.69 — below target.

Honest reflection

The gift box line overdelivered. The new product line underperformed primarily due to insufficient SKU uploads to the platform early in the campaign — an execution gap, not a targeting failure. The ROI ceiling was never identified because the campaign window ended before we could ramp spend high enough.

What I'd Do Differently

The hypothesis-driven audience targeting approach worked. Defining each segment's behavioral logic upfront, then making cut decisions based on real incremental ROI data, is the right framework. I'd keep that.

What I'd change: pre-define spend ramp criteria before launch instead of making those calls on intuition. In Round 2, the decision to aggressively increase daily caps came from recognizing the budget was underspent — but the threshold for "when to ramp" should have been set in advance as a data trigger (e.g., "if daily spend rate exceeds 80% of plan for 3 consecutive days, increase cap by 50%"), not a judgment call in the moment.

I'd also build SKU readiness verification into the pre-launch checklist. Several new product SKUs weren't live on the platform in the first days of the campaign, which capped the new product line's ceiling before it had a chance to fully ramp.
