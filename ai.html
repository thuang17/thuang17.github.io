How it started

Getting past the wall

First heard about ChatGPT and needed a workaround just to register — IP blocks, account restrictions, the full mainland China experience. Got help from some CS classmates who knew their way around it.

Once I had access, I used it mostly like a smarter search engine. Helped with some essays, answered questions, gave me information faster than Baidu. Useful, but not something I thought much about beyond that.

Things got serious

NotebookLM changes something

During my Decathlon internship, I accidentally ended up with a free year of Gemini Pro. Started using it for work — drafting, summarizing, translating between Chinese and English quickly.

Then I found NotebookLM. The concept: you feed it any materials — PDFs, documents, YouTube links — and it only works within what you give it. No hallucinations from the broader internet, just your sources. For research, for writing reports, for making sense of information you've collected — it clicked immediately.

This was the first time AI felt like a system rather than a feature.

Learning to communicate

Prompt training

When I started my master's at Sydney, I started using ChatGPT more deliberately — not just for outputs, but to understand how to ask better. What makes a good prompt. What context actually changes the result. How to get from a vague idea to something useful.

This is the part most people skip. I found it worth spending time on.

Discovering Claude

From chat tool to something else

I'd heard about Claude Code being popular for developers, but assumed it wasn't for me — I don't write code. Started using Claude as a regular chat assistant, same as everything else.

Then I started exploring Projects — long-running conversations with custom context. Then Claude Code, which I'd been avoiding. Somewhere in there I realized the thing I'd been worried about (not being technical enough) wasn't actually the barrier I thought it was.

Vibe coding

I made a product

My first project: a personal budget tracker. I wanted to build an app. Claude talked me into a web version instead — simpler stack, deployable immediately, works on any device.

I bought an Alibaba Cloud domain. Deployed it. The interface was rough. I didn't care at all.

Before this, I'd seen the full product development process from the inside at Decathlon: BRD, PRD, dev sprints, testing, UAT, stakeholder sign-off. A whole team, months of work. Here I'd done something complete — from idea to deployed product — in a weekend, on my own, without any of that formal structure.

That was the moment something changed in how I thought about what I was capable of building.

Vibe Coding Alibaba Cloud

Now

What's next

Personal website. Knowledge base. What comes after.

This website is the second thing I've built. Using Claude Code, same principle — describe what you want, iterate, deploy. It takes longer than I expected. It also produces something real.

I'm also building a personal knowledge base in Obsidian — a place to actually keep the ideas and observations that have been disappearing for years. I've had a lot of thoughts that seemed worth holding onto and then didn't. That's not a habit I want to continue.

I don't have a specific product I'm trying to build. I have a direction: understand these tools deeply enough that when I have an idea worth executing, the technical side isn't the thing that stops it.
