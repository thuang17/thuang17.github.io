The shift

AI stopped being a faster search box when I realized it could hold context, not just produce answers.

01

It started with access, not strategy.

First heard about ChatGPT and needed a workaround just to register: IP blocks, account restrictions, the full mainland China experience. Some CS classmates helped me get through the setup.

Once I had access, I used it like a smarter search engine. Essays, questions, quick explanations. Useful, but I did not yet see it as anything more serious than that.

ChatGPTSearch replacement
02

NotebookLM made the system visible.

During my Decathlon internship, I ended up with a free year of Gemini Pro and started using it for work: drafting, summarizing, and moving between Chinese and English quickly.

NotebookLM changed the mental model. Feed it PDFs, documents, and YouTube links, then make it answer from those sources. For research and report writing, that felt different from a chatbot. It was closer to a working memory.

Gemini ProNotebookLMSource-grounded work

"This was the first time AI felt like a system rather than a feature."

Turning point
03

I started treating prompts as thinking tools.

At Sydney, I became more deliberate with ChatGPT. Not just asking for outputs, but studying what changed the quality of the response: context, constraints, examples, tone, and the order of questions.

Most people skip that layer. I found it worth learning because the better I got at framing the problem, the more useful the tool became.

Prompt engineeringContext design
04

Claude moved it from conversation to execution.

I had heard Claude Code was popular with developers, but assumed it was not for me. I do not write code. I started with Claude as a normal chat assistant, then explored Projects and long-running context.

Claude Code was the thing I kept avoiding. Once I used it, I realized the barrier was not "I cannot code." The real barrier was whether I could describe the outcome clearly, review what changed, and keep iterating.

ClaudeClaude CodeProjects
05

The first product changed the question.

My first build was a personal budget tracker. I wanted an app. Claude pushed me toward a web version: simpler stack, deployable immediately, usable on any device.

I bought an Alibaba Cloud domain and deployed it. The interface was rough. I did not care. What mattered was that I had gone from idea to shipped product in a weekend, on my own.

At Decathlon, I had seen product work as BRD, PRD, dev sprints, testing, UAT, and stakeholder sign-off. This was smaller, messier, and much faster. It made building feel reachable.

What changed
06

Now I am building a personal operating system.

This website is the second thing I have built with Claude Code. Same principle: describe the intent, inspect the result, correct the parts that feel wrong, and deploy.

I am also building a personal knowledge base in Obsidian because too many useful observations have disappeared after I failed to capture them. The goal is not to become a software engineer overnight. It is to understand these tools deeply enough that the technical side does not stop a good idea before it starts.

Operating principles

How I use AI now

Context first

I try to feed the model the right source material before asking for output. Bad context makes confident noise.

Iterate in public

I do not expect a perfect first answer. I inspect, correct, and keep the loop tight until the thing works.

Ship small

The point is not to make a grand product immediately. It is to keep converting vague ideas into real artifacts.
