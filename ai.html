AI Explorer

Learning to build before calling myself technical

I did not come from engineering. AI started as a workaround — getting past IP blocks and account restrictions in mainland China just to try ChatGPT. Useful as a smarter search engine, but nothing more.

Over time it became a way to think, organize context, and ship small products without waiting for permission from my own technical insecurity.

01

NotebookLM made the system visible.

During my Decathlon internship, I used Gemini Pro for drafting and translation. But NotebookLM changed the mental model: feed it PDFs and documents, make it answer from those sources. For research and report writing, that felt closer to working memory than a chatbot.

02

I started treating prompts as thinking tools.

At Sydney, I became more deliberate. Not just asking for outputs, but studying what changed the quality of the response: context, constraints, examples, tone, and the order of questions. Most people skip that layer. I found it worth learning.

"This was the first time AI felt like a system rather than a feature."

Turning point
03

Claude moved it from conversation to execution.

I had heard Claude Code was popular with developers but assumed it wasn't for me. I started with normal chat, then Projects and long-running context. The barrier wasn't "I can't code." It was whether I could describe the outcome clearly, review what changed, and keep iterating.

04

The first product changed the question.

My first build was a personal budget tracker. Claude pushed me toward a web version: simpler stack, deployable immediately. I bought a domain and shipped it in a weekend. At Decathlon, product work meant BRDs, PRDs, dev sprints, UAT, stakeholder sign-off. This was smaller, messier, much faster. It made building feel reachable.

"I am not a coder. But 'I cannot build things' is no longer how I think."

What changed
05

Now I'm building a personal operating system.

This website is the second thing I've built with Claude Code. Same principle: describe the intent, inspect the result, correct what feels wrong, deploy. I'm also building a personal knowledge base in Obsidian — too many useful observations have disappeared because I failed to capture them. The goal isn't to become a software engineer overnight. It's to understand these tools deeply enough that the technical side doesn't stop a good idea before it starts.

How I use AI now

Context first

Feed the model the right source material before asking for output. Bad context makes confident noise.

Iterate in public

Don't expect a perfect first answer. Inspect, correct, keep the loop tight until it works.

Ship small

The point isn't to make a grand product. It's to keep converting vague ideas into real artifacts.
