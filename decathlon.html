Context

Decathlon's China e-commerce team runs across several channels simultaneously — WeChat Mini Program, Douyin, Tmall, Taobao Flash Sale. The PM team sits between business and IT: they gather requirements from the business side, translate them into specs, coordinate development, and manage testing before launch.

I was embedded in this team for three months. My mentor gave me a fairly open brief: learn the system, find problems, contribute where you can. That open-endedness turned out to define how the most interesting parts of the internship went.

Three areas of work: a UX improvement project I initiated myself, UAT coordination across roughly eight concurrent projects, and an internal PM tool optimization.

WeChat Mini Program UX Project

How it started

Nobody assigned me this. In the first few weeks, I spent time using the Decathlon WeChat Mini Program as a regular customer would — browsing, adding to cart, checking logistics, going through the returns flow. A few things kept bothering me, so I started writing them down.

When I had enough observations to make a structured case, I brought it to my mentor. His response: if you think it's worth pursuing, build the analysis and we'll see if it justifies a project proposal.

Research approach

No internal user behavior data was available — no heatmaps, no conversion funnel breakdowns. I flagged this to my mentor; his judgment was that the pain points were visible enough to proceed without it.

So I used competitive benchmarking instead: full-funnel walkthroughs of JD 1P, JD 3P, and Tmall, covering the product detail page, logistics tracking, and the after-sales and returns flow. Documented everything with screenshots and written notes.

Identified 16 pain points in total. Key examples:

  • SKU naming inconsistency — product names on the Mini Program didn't match what content creators were using off-platform, breaking the seeding-to-conversion flow
  • Missing logistics ETA — no estimated delivery time displayed after purchase, a friction point that's low-cost to fix and high-impact for customer confidence
  • No self-service return with courier pickup — customers had to contact customer service manually for returns, while JD had fully automated this

Priority framework

With 16 pain points and limited development bandwidth, I needed a way to prioritize that both I and the team could agree on. I built a customized Value-Effort matrix with dual-dimension weighted scoring:

  • Consumer need intensity (0–5): how much does this actually affect the user's decision to buy or return?
  • Business value (0–5): what's the estimated impact on conversion, return rate, or operational cost?

Ran two alignment meetings with the team's PM to validate the quadrant placements before finalizing. High-priority items went into the sprint; others into the backlog.

Key decision

My mentor initially wanted to prioritize self-service returns with courier pickup. I pushed back — not because I disagreed with the end goal, but on timing. The feature requires reverse logistics API integration and courier partnership agreements: short-term build cost is high, and adding a frictionless return path in the short term would likely spike return rates before those costs are recouped. We agreed it belonged in the long-term roadmap, not the current sprint.

Output

All 16 pain points mapped to the Value-Effort matrix, with the top-priority items written up as formal requirements. Also drafted a BRD for a separate initiative: expanding Mini Program delivery radius from 3km to 10km, with a projected annual GMV uplift of 6M+ RMB.

UAT Coordination

While the UX project was running, I was also involved in UAT (User Acceptance Testing) coordination across about eight concurrent projects — spanning DTC manual refunds, two new Douyin stores (kids + apparel/footwear), Tmall, and Taobao Flash Sale.

The role was essentially information routing: track what tests are scheduled, record issues found, get the right people aligned across business, IT, finance, and customer service, and follow up until every issue was closed.

When it got complicated

On the DTC manual refund project, Round 1 testing was done and IT had completed their fixes. The customer service team was assigned Round 2 re-testing with a clear deadline. The deadline passed with no response to WeChat messages.

I decided async wasn't working. Walked over and scheduled a short in-person meeting — but didn't open with "you're overdue." I asked about their current situation first, then explained what the project would actually do for them: standardizing the refund process would reduce their compliance exposure and cut the manual workload they were dealing with on every price-difference case.

They hadn't thought about it from that angle. Re-testing was completed promptly afterward.

What I took from this

When cross-team alignment stalls, the problem is usually one of incentives, not communication channel. Switching from async to in-person was useful, but more useful was leading with "here's what this does for you" rather than "here's what you're supposed to do by when."

PM Tool Optimization

Decathlon was building an internal PM tool rather than buying a commercial one — the logic being that their specific workflow didn't map cleanly onto Jira or Monday. My mentor, knowing I was studying project management, assigned me to benchmark the internal tool against what good PM software actually does.

What I found

Benchmarked against Jira, Trello, Monday, DingTalk, and Feishu. Identified 13 pain points in the internal tool. Key ones:

  • No project progress visibility without clicking through to an external Jira link
  • No clear owner mapping — couldn't tell who was responsible for a project without asking around
  • Dashboard number cards lacked context (just a number, no trend or comparison)
  • Milestone hover states not showing detail
  • Filter logic inconsistencies (select-all / reset / multi-select behaved differently)

Wrote up a complete requirements iteration document and aligned it with my manager.

Trello introduction

Separately, I noticed the team was using Notion for team-level task tracking and finding it insufficient (free tier limitations, not designed for kanban workflows). I proposed Trello as a lightweight complement — not a replacement for the internal PM tool, which handles company-wide project management, but for day-to-day team task tracking.

Walked through the tradeoffs with my mentor, who approved. The team adopted it and continued using it through my tenure.

Reflection

The UX project is the one I'd spend more time on if I did this again. The competitive benchmarking approach was useful, but I should have pushed harder to get any internal data — even partial conversion funnel data would have strengthened the priority arguments significantly. "The pain points are obvious enough" is a reasonable pragmatic call, but it leaves the prioritization open to challenge.

The UAT coordination work was valuable in a different way. Managing eight concurrent projects across four or five stakeholder groups in a large organization is messier than it looks on a Gantt chart. Most of the actual work was keeping information current and making sure the right people knew what they needed to know. When that breaks down — as it did with the CS team — the fix is almost never "send a clearer message." It's usually about changing the frame.
