Darts

Got in by accident. Stayed because it made me better.

01

The lottery

First year at SUIBE, I tried to sign up for archery as my PE elective. Didn't get it. Got darts instead. For the first few sessions, it was just something to fill a requirement. Then it wasn't.

02

Deliberate practice

I started staying after sessions to practice alone. Watched professional players — studied their stance, their grip, their follow-through. Then went back to the board and tried to fix one thing at a time.

What got me was the precision of it. Every throw is fully repeatable. Every inconsistency is yours to own.

03

An organization in name only

The SUIBE Darts Association had been nationally ranked in 2019. Then COVID hit, the club went quiet for two years, and by the time things reopened the structure had mostly dissolved.

Somehow I became president. I didn't inherit an organization so much as a general idea of one — about 50 people with varying levels of commitment, no formal school team, no event calendar. The first thing I did was rebuild the structure. Added a dedicated events department and a PR/outreach department. People thinking about competitions, people thinking about recruitment, separately.

04

From 50 to 100, and a real team

Within two years the club grew from about 50 to nearly 100 members. We rebuilt the school team from scratch — proper selection, regular training sessions, a real lineup at competitions.

I also wanted us to look like we belonged when we showed up to compete. So I designed new team uniforms: black base, red and white graphics, "SUIBE DARTS" across the front. Both men's and women's cuts.

05

The Shanghai University League

The competition I remember most is the Shanghai University Darts League, hosted at ECUPL. Multiple schools, proper format, individual and team events.

I won individual first. Our team finished third. I always get nervous on the finishing throws — hands tighten, everything slows down. That match I was nervous. Threw anyway.

06

Try to be a real player

Dartslive is a global network of ranked darts machines with organized tournaments. I registered a card and started competing in their Shanghai events. The players there were in a completely different league — regulars who'd been playing for years, way faster on the finishes. I made it past round one maybe once or twice.

That was fine. What I actually looked forward to: finishing a tournament at midnight and heading to a nearby noodle place with people I'd just played against. Or the late-night sessions across town at a venue with more machines, better lighting, and — I'm not making this up — a folding bed someone had dragged in because we kept playing until 11pm.

The friends from other schools, the club leaders from across Shanghai — those connections came directly from darts. Without it, university would have been a lot smaller.

07

Finding the next president

Before I left, I needed to find a successor. I wanted someone who genuinely cared about the game, not just the title.

I found a junior who'd been playing seriously since high school — technically strong, good at bringing people together. We competed together, trained together, spent enough time in the same venues that it felt like a real handoff rather than an administrative one. I left knowing the club would be fine.
